## TL;DR

**Choose BigSpy if:** You want generalist cross-platform ad research across Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, Twitter, TikTok, and Pinterest, and you want the lowest entry price in the category. Free tier, Basic $9/mo, Pro $99/mo, VIP Enterprise $249/mo (as of April 2026). Best for first-time buyers, generalist agencies, and brand teams watching trends broadly.

**Choose Minea if:** You run dropshipping or DTC e-commerce and need product research, supplier hints, and visibility into Facebook, TikTok, Pinterest, and influencer ads. Minea Starter €49/mo, Premium €99/mo, Business €399/mo (as of April 2026). Free tier available. Best for Shopify operators, product hunters, and DTC brands working across short-form and social commerce.

These are different tools for different buyers. BigSpy sells generalist breadth. Minea sells e-commerce depth. Both publish free tiers, which lowers the commitment to try either.

If your actual job is CPA affiliate research on Meta — ClickBank, Digistore24, BuyGoods — neither is the right fit. AdRecon is built for that specific workflow, and we flag it honestly.

## Where BigSpy wins

**Six-platform coverage in one seat.** Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, Twitter, TikTok, Pinterest. Minea covers roughly four surfaces (Meta, TikTok, Pinterest, influencer). For researchers who need YouTube or Twitter ad visibility alongside Meta and TikTok, BigSpy is the only option between these two.

**Lowest entry price in the category.** BigSpy Basic at $9/mo (as of April 2026) and a free tier on top make it the cheapest ad-spy tool on the market. Minea Starter at €49/mo is meaningfully more expensive — roughly $55 at recent exchange rates, about 6x BigSpy Basic's monthly cost.

**USD-native pricing.** No currency conversion, no EUR renewal math. For US-based operators, BigSpy's USD pricing is easier to budget than Minea's EUR tiers, which fluctuate slightly at each renewal depending on exchange rates.

**Neutral across verticals.** BigSpy doesn't assume your use case — general research UX serves affiliates, brand teams, e-commerce, SaaS marketers, and agencies equally. Minea's UI assumes product-and-supplier context; if you're not doing e-commerce research, some of that context is noise.

**Higher query limits at upper tier.** VIP Enterprise at $249/mo unlocks generous search and export volume suited to larger research teams. Minea Business at €399/mo is positioned more as an "e-commerce team" plan with dropshipping-specific features than as raw research throughput.

**Faster onboarding for non-e-commerce teams.** BigSpy's neutral UI and six-platform coverage make it usable within an hour. Minea's e-commerce-specific UI — product cards, supplier tabs, niche tags — assumes domain fluency. For a generalist marketing team or first-time buyer unfamiliar with dropshipping workflows, BigSpy's learning curve is meaningfully shorter.

## Where Minea wins

**E-commerce and dropshipping-specific features.** Minea was built for product research — supplier hints, product metadata enrichment, Shopify-adjacent tooling, and niche-level filtering for e-commerce verticals. BigSpy is a general ad-spy tool without these features. For dropshippers hunting winning products, Minea's lens is the correct one.

**Influencer ad coverage.** Minea tracks influencer-driven ads as a separate surface, which matters for DTC brands running creator campaigns and affiliate operators partnering with influencers. BigSpy treats influencer content as part of general platform indexing rather than a dedicated surface.

**Better UX for visual product browsing.** Minea's product cards, niche tags, and media-first layout make scanning dozens of candidate products fast. BigSpy's more neutral UI requires more clicks to get similar visual context. For product hunters evaluating 50+ items per session, the UX gap compounds.

**Tighter feedback loop on winning products.** Minea surfaces engagement signals (likes, shares, ad duration) in a way tuned to product validation — essentially answering "is this product scaling?" BigSpy surfaces engagement too, but the interpretation layer for product research is thinner.

**TikTok and Pinterest depth tuned for e-commerce.** Minea's TikTok and Pinterest crawling prioritizes product-oriented content. BigSpy's crawling is more general, mixing brand, service, and product ads. For e-commerce-specific research on those two surfaces, Minea tends to feel cleaner.

## Pricing head-to-head

**BigSpy:** Free tier + Basic $9/mo + Pro $99/mo + VIP Enterprise $249/mo (as of April 2026). Year-one: $0 (free) to $2,988 (VIP Enterprise).

**Minea:** Starter €49/mo + Premium €99/mo + Business €399/mo (as of April 2026). Free tier for tasting. Year-one: €588 to €4,788 — roughly $635 to $5,200 at recent exchange rates.

At the entry level, BigSpy Basic ($9/mo) vs Minea Starter (~$55/mo) is a 6x gap. For operators on a tight budget, BigSpy Basic wins that math decisively — but you're buying different products. BigSpy Basic is generalist research. Minea Starter is e-commerce research with supplier hints.

At the mid tier, BigSpy Pro ($99/mo) vs Minea Premium (~$107/mo) is the cleanest apples-to-apples comparison. Both are in the $100/mo range. Pro gives you six platforms with general research features. Premium gives you four e-commerce-tuned surfaces with dropshipping-specific tooling. Which tier matches your workflow decides the fit.

At the top tier, Minea Business at €399/mo is roughly $430/mo — about 70% more than BigSpy VIP Enterprise's $249/mo. Business is priced for serious e-commerce agencies with specific feature needs. For generalist research, VIP Enterprise is the better value.

## The affiliate marketer question — is either the right fit?

For CPA affiliate research specifically, neither one is built for the job.

Neither auto-classifies affiliate networks. ClickBank, Digistore24, BuyGoods, MaxWeb, WarriorPlus — none appear as filterable fields in BigSpy or Minea. You're stuck pasting domains into search and hoping they survive the next subdomain rotation.

Neither archives complete landing pages. BigSpy shows destination URLs. Minea shows destination URLs plus e-commerce metadata. Neither captures the full HTML/CSS/assets of the funnel as a downloadable archive. When the LP goes down tomorrow or the advertiser changes the funnel, the page is gone.

Neither groups creative concepts across independent advertisers with proof thresholds. If fifteen different Meta advertisers run substantially the same creative, both tools show you fifteen separate ads. There's no "proven concept" filter that surfaces creative ideas validated by multiple independent operators.

AdRecon was built for that job on Meta. Meta-only (Facebook, Instagram, Threads — no TikTok, YouTube, Pinterest, or Twitter), 300,000+ records across ads, landing pages, creatives, and offers, regex-based auto-classification of ClickBank / Digistore24 / BuyGoods / MaxWeb / WarriorPlus, Landing Page Ripper that captures complete HTML/CSS/assets as ZIP, and proven-creative groupings at 15+/30+/60+/90+ linked-ad thresholds. $299 lifetime.

Honest tradeoff: AdRecon doesn't cover dropshipping product hunting (Minea's job) or cross-platform generalist research (BigSpy's job). If your research is Meta affiliate intelligence, AdRecon fits directly. If it isn't, one of the two tools above is probably the better pick.

## Final verdict

BigSpy and Minea don't really compete for the same buyer. BigSpy is a generalist cross-platform research tool priced for broad use cases and first-time buyers. Minea is an e-commerce specialist priced for dropshippers and DTC operators who need product and supplier research alongside ad visibility.

If you're not doing e-commerce research, BigSpy is the cleaner choice. The free tier lowers the risk to try, six-platform coverage is genuinely broader, and USD pricing is simpler for US operators.

If you are doing e-commerce research, Minea is the clearer winner. The product-research lens, supplier hints, and influencer-ad surface all serve dropshipping workflows in ways BigSpy's generalist UI doesn't. Premium at €99/mo is reasonable for the capability.

For CPA affiliates on Meta, we keep pointing to AdRecon — neither tool above classifies networks or archives landing pages, and those are the two jobs that actually drive affiliate research forward.
