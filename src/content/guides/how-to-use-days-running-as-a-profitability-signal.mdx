## TL;DR
* **The Core Heuristic:** An ad running for 90+ days is likely profitable; an ad running for 120+ days is almost certainly a "winner" that has survived multiple optimization cycles.
* **The Testing Window:** Ignore ads running for less than 14 days. These are in the "testing phase" and have not yet proven their ROI.
* **The False Positive Filter:** Long run-times can be misleading if the advertiser is a massive brand with a "loss leader" strategy. Focus on solo affiliates or mid-sized agencies.
* **The Seasonal Reset:** Check for "historical ghosts"—ads that reappear every Q4 or summer. A 10-day-old ad that looks identical to a 200-day-old ad from last year is a high-conviction play.
* **The Network Signal:** Use affiliate-network classification to ensure the "winner" is on a network you can actually join (e.g., ClickBank or MaxWeb).
* **The Creative Decay:** When a 120-day winner starts to fade, it's the perfect time to "rip" the landing page and iterate on the creative to beat the original.

## Why this matters
In the high-stakes world of CPA (Cost Per Acquisition) affiliate marketing, the most expensive mistake you can make is "testing" a dead offer. Many beginners spend hundreds of dollars in ad spend trying to "crack" an offer that simply doesn't convert. They treat the ad platform as a laboratory, hoping that a slight tweak in the headline or a different image will magically make a bad product sell.

The professional operator does the opposite. They don't look for "potential"; they look for "proof." The most reliable proof available in the public domain is the "Days Running" signal. Because Meta ads operate on an auction system where costs can scale rapidly, no sane affiliate will keep an ad active for 120 days if it is losing money. The math is simple: if an ad has been spending budget for four months, the advertiser is getting a return on that investment.

By shifting your workflow from "testing" to "modeling proven winners," you eliminate the gambling aspect of affiliate marketing. Instead of guessing which offer might work, you identify the offers that *are* working and build a superior version of that funnel. This saves thousands of dollars in wasted spend and hundreds of hours of fruitless optimization.

## Prerequisites and assumptions
Before implementing this workflow, you should have a basic understanding of how Meta's ad auction works and a functioning account with at least one affiliate network (such as ClickBank, Digistore24, BuyGoods, MaxWeb, or WarriorPlus). You should also be comfortable with the concept of a "bridge page" or "presell page," as you will be analyzing these to understand why a long-running ad is successful.

Technically, you will need a spy tool that provides a "first seen" or "days active" metric. Not all spy tools treat this data with the same granularity. Some only tell you when they first scraped the ad, while others provide a more accurate delivery window. For this guide, we assume you are using a tool that allows you to filter by a specific number of days an ad has been active.

## The workflow / framework

### Step 1: Filter for the "Stability Zone"
The first step is to strip away the noise. The Meta ad ecosystem is flooded with "churn and burn" ads—low-quality creatives that run for 48 hours, get banned, or fail miserably. If you search for "weight loss" without a time filter, you will see thousands of ads. 95% of these are irrelevant.

You must apply a "Days Running" filter. For high-conviction research, set your filter to **Minimum 60 days**. While 30 days is a common benchmark, 60 days indicates that the ad has survived at least two monthly billing cycles and has likely weathered the "learning phase" of the Meta algorithm. If you are looking for "Evergreen" winners, push this filter to **120 days**.

When performing this search, use a tool that allows you to isolate specific affiliate networks. For example, if you are a ClickBank affiliate, filtering for "ClickBank" + "120 Days Running" immediately narrows your field from 10,000 random ads to the top 1% of proven offers.

### Step 2: Identify the "Creative Cluster"
Once you have a list of ads running for 120+ days, do not just pick one. Look for "clusters." A cluster occurs when multiple different advertisers are running nearly identical creatives or landing pages for the same offer.

If one person has an ad running for 150 days, it might be a fluke or a very specific audience segment. But if five different affiliates all have ads running for 90+ days for the same "Keto" supplement, you have found a "Winning Offer." This indicates that the offer's conversion rate is high enough to support multiple competitors in the same auction.

Analyze the commonalities in these clusters:
* **The Hook:** Are they all using a "weird trick" angle?
* **The Visual:** Are they all using "ugly" user-generated content (UGC) or polished studio shots?
* **The CTA:** Are they sending traffic to a VSL (Video Sales Letter) or a quiz?

### Step 3: Analyze the Landing Page Architecture
A long-running ad is only half the equation. The "profitability signal" is actually a combination of the Ad $\rightarrow$ Landing Page $\rightarrow$ Offer. If the ad has been running for 120 days, the landing page is the engine driving that longevity.

You need to dissect the landing page. Look for the "Conversion Triggers":
* **The Lead:** How do they grab attention in the first 3 seconds?
* **The Proof:** What kind of testimonials or scientific claims are they using?
* **The Friction:** Is it a long-form sales page or a short "bridge" page?

To do this efficiently, you shouldn't just browse the page in a browser. You need to see the underlying structure. Using a "Landing Page Ripper" allows you to download the HTML and CSS, letting you see exactly how the page is built, what tracking scripts are being used, and how the mobile responsiveness is handled.

### Step 4: Cross-Reference with Offer Demand
Before you launch, verify that the offer is still "healthy" on the affiliate network. A long-running ad can sometimes be a "zombie ad"—an ad that is still running because the budget is low, but the offer has since been capped or the payouts have dropped.

Go to your network (e.g., MaxWeb or BuyGoods) and check the current top-performing offers. If the offer you found in the spy tool is also listed as a "Top Offer" or "Trending" on the network, the signal is confirmed. If the offer is no longer available or has a terrible payout, discard it, regardless of how many days the ad has been running.

### Step 5: Iterate and "Out-Model" the Winner
The final step is not to copy the 120-day winner, but to improve upon it. By the time an ad has run for 120 days, it is likely experiencing "creative fatigue." The audience has seen it, and the Click-Through Rate (CTR) is probably dropping.

This is your competitive advantage. You have the blueprint of a proven winner, but you can launch a "fresh" version. 
1. **Keep the Hook:** Use the same core psychological angle that worked for 120 days.
2. **Refresh the Visual:** Create a new video or image that delivers that hook more effectively.
3. **Optimize the LP:** Take the ripped landing page and improve the load speed or add a more compelling headline.

By doing this, you are entering the auction with a "proven" strategy but "fresh" assets, which often leads to a lower CPA than the original advertiser.

## Worked example
Imagine an operator searching for a winner in the "Health/Supplements" niche on the BuyGoods network. 

They set their spy tool filter to: **Network: BuyGoods | Days Running: 120+ | Platform: Instagram**. 

They find a specific "Blood Sugar" supplement. One ad has been running for 142 days. Upon further inspection, they find four other advertisers running similar "blood sugar" ads, all with run-times between 80 and 110 days. This is a high-conviction cluster.

The operator rips the landing page of the 142-day winner and notices it's a simple quiz funnel. They check BuyGoods and see the offer is still paying $120 per sale and is currently in the top 5 for the niche. 

Instead of copying the ad, the operator creates three new UGC-style videos using the same "quiz" hook. They launch these against the same target audience. Because the "quiz" angle is proven to work (the 142-day signal), and the creative is fresh, they achieve a 3% CTR compared to the estimated 1.2% the original advertiser is likely seeing after four months of fatigue.

## Pitfalls to avoid

### The "Brand Budget" Trap
The biggest mistake is assuming every long-running ad is profitable for an *affiliate*. Large brands (like Nike or established supplement companies) often run "Brand Awareness" campaigns. They might run an ad for 365 days not because it's making a direct profit on every click, but because it keeps their brand top-of-mind. If you see a massive corporate brand running an ad for a year, do not assume that same funnel will work for a solo affiliate. Look for ads run by "Marketing LLCs" or individual affiliate accounts.

### Ignoring the "Learning Phase"
Beginners often get excited by an ad that has been running for 10 or 15 days. They see a "cool creative" and assume it's a winner. In reality, 15 days is often just the "testing phase." Many affiliates set a budget for two weeks to see if an offer has any legs. If the ad is killed on day 16, it was a failure. Never treat an ad under 30 days as a "proven" signal.

### The Seasonal Blindspot
If you are researching in October, a "120-day winner" might have started in June. If that offer is a "Summer Weight Loss" product, the 120-day signal is irrelevant for November. You must distinguish between "Evergreen" offers (which work year-round) and "Seasonal" offers. For seasonal offers, the "Days Running" signal only matters if you compare it to the same window in the previous year.

### Over-Reliance on One Metric
Days-running is a powerful signal, but it's not a magic bullet. If an ad has been running for 200 days but the landing page is broken, the offer is out of stock, or the payout has been slashed to pennies, the "profitability" is gone. Always verify the "back end" of the funnel (the offer and the network) before committing budget.

### The "Copy-Paste" Fallacy
Copying a 120-day winner exactly is a recipe for failure. Not only is the creative likely fatigued, but Meta's algorithm can sometimes penalize identical content. The goal of using the days-running signal is to identify the *psychology* that works, not the *pixels*. Use the signal to find the "What" and the "Why," then execute your own "How."
