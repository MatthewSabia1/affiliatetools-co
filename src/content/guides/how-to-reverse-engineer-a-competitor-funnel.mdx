## TL;DR
* **Identify the "Winner":** Use a spy tool to find ads running for 30+ days; longevity is the only reliable proxy for profitability.
* **Classify the Offer:** Determine if the offer is from a major network (ClickBank, MaxWeb, etc.) to understand the payout structure and restrictions.
* **Rip the Assets:** Download the full HTML/CSS/JS of the landing page to analyze the exact layout and trigger points without guessing.
* **Map the Path:** Trace the journey from Ad $\rightarrow$ Opt-in $\rightarrow$ Bridge Page $\rightarrow$ VSL $\rightarrow$ Checkout.
* **Deconstruct the Hook:** Isolate the specific angle (e.g., "The Secret Ritual") and test a variation of it.
* **Audit the Post-Click:** Analyze the thank-you page and email sequence to see how the competitor maximizes LTV.

## Why this matters
Most affiliate marketers approach "research" as a glancing exercise. They find a winning ad, look at the landing page for thirty seconds, and then try to recreate it from memory using a drag-and-drop builder. This naive approach fails because it misses the "invisible" elements of a high-converting funnel: the specific CSS triggers, the exact loading sequence of the VSL, the hidden tracking scripts, and the precise wording of the micro-copy.

When you guess, you leak money. A misplaced button or a slow-loading image can drop your conversion rate by 20%—which, at scale, represents thousands of dollars in lost commissions. Reverse engineering is not about plagiarism; it is about removing the guesswork from the equation. It allows you to start your testing phase at the "baseline of success" rather than starting from zero.

For a professional operator, the goal is to identify the *mechanism* of the conversion. Why is this specific bridge page working? Is it the social proof? The countdown timer? The specific way the lead magnet is framed? By ripping the page and analyzing the raw assets, you can see exactly how the competitor is manipulating the user's psychology to drive them toward the offer.

## Prerequisites and assumptions
Before starting this workflow, you should have a basic understanding of the affiliate marketing ecosystem—specifically the difference between a vendor, an affiliate network, and a media buyer. You should be comfortable with the concept of a "bridge page" (a page that sits between the ad and the offer to warm up the lead or collect an email).

In terms of tooling, you will need a dedicated ad intelligence tool to find the creatives and a way to host your own pages for testing. If you are targeting Meta (Facebook/Instagram), you will need a tool that can archive landing pages, as competitors often take their pages down or change them the moment they detect a surge in "spy" traffic.

## The workflow / framework

### Step 1: Isolate the Profitable Creative
The first mistake beginners make is searching for "cool" ads. Professionals search for "profitable" ads. The only metric that matters in a spy tool is the **days-running** signal. If an ad has been active for 14, 30, or 60 days, the operator is making money. No one spends $100/day for a month on an ad that doesn't convert.

Set your filters to exclude any ad that has been running for less than 14 days. If you are using a tool with affiliate-network classification, filter by the network you are currently promoting (e.g., ClickBank or BuyGoods). This narrows the field from millions of generic ads to the few hundred that are actually making money in your specific vertical.

In this step, you are looking for "Proven Creatives." Look for patterns: are multiple different advertisers using the same image? If three different affiliates are all using a photo of a "strange household fruit" to sell a weight loss offer, you have found a winning angle. Document the ad copy, the headline, and the specific call to action (CTA).

### Step 2: Classify the Offer and Network
Once you have a winning ad, you need to know who is paying the commission. This tells you the "gravity" of the offer. If the offer is on a major network like MaxWeb or Digistore24, you can look up the offer's current rankings and payout terms. This helps you determine if the offer is still "hot" or if it's starting to saturate.

Navigate to the landing page from the ad. Look at the URL structure. Many affiliates use tracking sub-IDs that reveal the network. For example, seeing "cb" in a URL often points to ClickBank. If you use a tool that automatically classifies the network, this step takes seconds; otherwise, you'll have to manually inspect the outbound links on the page.

Understanding the network also allows you to check the "Affiliate Terms." Some vendors forbid certain types of bridge pages or specific keywords. By knowing the network, you can ensure your reverse-engineered funnel doesn't get your account banned before you even launch.

### Step 3: Rip the Landing Page Assets
This is where most marketers stop, and where professionals accelerate. Simply looking at a page in a browser is insufficient because many high-converting pages use "cloaking" or "dynamic content" that changes based on the visitor's IP or device. To truly analyze a funnel, you need the raw code.

Use a "Landing Page Ripper" to download the entire page as a ZIP file. This includes the HTML, the CSS stylesheets, the JavaScript files, and all image assets. By having the local files, you can open the code in a text editor (like VS Code) and see exactly how the page is structured. You can find hidden tracking pixels, see which external scripts are being loaded for optimization, and identify the exact fonts and colors used to create the "trust" signal.

Once you have the ZIP, upload it to a staging server. This allows you to interact with the page in a controlled environment. You can now see exactly how the page behaves on mobile versus desktop without worrying about the competitor's server blocking your IP or redirecting you to a different offer.

### Step 4: Map the Post-Click Journey
A funnel is rarely just one page. It is a sequence of psychological shifts. You must map every single step from the moment the user clicks the ad to the moment they reach the "Thank You" page. 

1. **The Ad:** What was the promise? (e.g., "Lose 10lbs in 2 weeks").
2. **The Bridge Page/Opt-in:** Does it ask for an email first? Does it use a quiz? Does it use a "pre-sell" article?
3. **The VSL (Video Sales Letter):** How long is the video? When does the "Buy" button appear? (Many high-converting VSLs hide the button until a specific timestamp).
4. **The Checkout:** What are the order bumps? What is the primary price point?
5. **The Upsell/Downsell:** What happens after the first purchase? This is where the real profit is made.

Document this flow in a flowchart. Note the "friction points"—the places where the user is asked to do something difficult (like enter a credit card). See how the competitor reduces that friction through copy or design.

### Step 5: Deconstruct the Hook and Angle
Now that you have the technical blueprint, you need the psychological blueprint. The "Hook" is the specific reason the user stopped scrolling. The "Angle" is the narrative used to bridge the gap between the hook and the offer.

For example, if the hook is "The 30-second morning ritual," the angle might be "Modern medicine ignores this ancient secret." You are not looking to copy the words, but to identify the *category* of the angle. Is it a "Secret/Discovery" angle? A "Fear/Urgency" angle? Or a "Case Study/Proof" angle?

Once you identify the angle, create three variations of it. If the competitor is using a "Secret Ritual" angle, try a "Forgotten Method" angle or a "Doctor's Warning" angle. By using the competitor's proven structure but changing the narrative "skin," you avoid direct plagiarism while still leveraging a proven psychological trigger.

## Worked example
Imagine you are in the "Health/Weight Loss" niche. You use a spy tool and find an ad for a ketogenic supplement that has been running for 42 days. The ad features a simple image of a glass of water with lemon and a caption about a "morning trick."

You use the Landing Page Ripper to download the bridge page. Upon inspecting the HTML, you realize the page is incredibly lightweight—no heavy images, just fast-loading text and a single "Watch Video" button. This tells you the competitor is optimizing for mobile speed to reduce bounce rates.

You follow the funnel: Ad $\rightarrow$ Simple Bridge Page $\rightarrow$ 12-minute VSL $\rightarrow$ Checkout with a "Buy 3 Get 2 Free" bundle. You notice the VSL button only appears at the 11:45 mark. You decide to build a similar lightweight bridge page but change the "morning trick" angle to a "nightly habit" angle, keeping the same high-speed technical structure and the delayed-button VSL strategy.

## Pitfalls to avoid

### The "Blind Copy" Trap
The biggest mistake is importing a ripped page and running traffic to it without changes. Not only is this often a violation of network terms, but it also ignores the "creative fatigue" factor. If the competitor's page has been running for 60 days, the audience may already be tired of that exact layout. Always change the imagery and the primary headline to refresh the offer for the user.

### Ignoring Page Speed
Many affiliates use heavy page builders (like Elementor or Divi) to recreate a ripped page. In doing so, they add 2MB of unnecessary bloat. If the competitor's ripped page is 200KB of clean HTML/CSS, and your "recreation" is 3MB, your conversion rate will plummet regardless of how good the copy is. Always prioritize speed over fancy animations.

### Overlooking the Mobile Experience
Most Meta traffic is mobile. Some operators analyze funnels on a 27-inch iMac and forget that 95% of their customers are on an iPhone 13. When reverse engineering, use the "Inspect" tool in Chrome to view the page at mobile dimensions. Check if the "Buy" button is "above the fold" on a mobile screen. If the competitor has a sticky footer button on mobile, you must implement one too.

### Neglecting the Upsell Path
Beginners focus entirely on the front-end conversion. However, in high-ticket or supplement affiliate marketing, the front-end offer often just breaks even. The profit is in the upsells. If you don't reverse engineer the post-purchase experience (the "Thank You" page and the immediate upsell offers), you are leaving 50-70% of your potential revenue on the table.

### Trusting "New" Ads
Avoid the temptation to copy an ad that was launched yesterday and has a huge amount of engagement (likes/comments). This is often a sign of a "burn-and-turn" campaign or a high-budget test that may not actually be profitable. Stick to the longevity signal. A boring ad that has run for 30 days is infinitely more valuable than a flashy ad that has run for 2 days.

## How to know the workflow is working
You will know this workflow is paying off when your "Time to First Sale" decreases. Instead of spending two weeks testing five different landing page layouts, you should be able to launch a "baseline" funnel that converts at a known rate within 48 hours.

The ultimate signal is your **CPA (Cost Per Acquisition) stability**. When you reverse engineer a proven funnel, you eliminate the "wild swings" in performance. You aren't guessing if the page works; you know the structure works, so you can focus your energy entirely on optimizing the ad creative and the offer angle.
