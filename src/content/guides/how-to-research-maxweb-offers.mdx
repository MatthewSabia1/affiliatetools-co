## TL;DR
* **Isolate the Network:** Don't search for "weight loss"; filter specifically for MaxWeb-classified ads to see what the network's top earners are pushing.
* **The 14-Day Rule:** Ignore any ad running for less than two weeks. Profitability is proven by longevity, not by the "flashiness" of the creative.
* **Rip, Don't Copy:** Use landing page archives to analyze the conversion psychology (hooks, testimonials, scarcity) rather than blindly copying HTML.
* **Cross-Reference Creatives:** Identify "Proven Creatives" by finding the same image or video being used across multiple different advertisers for the same offer.
* **Verify the Funnel:** Always click through to the final offer page to ensure the MaxWeb offer is still active and hasn't been paused or capped.

## Why this matters
MaxWeb has emerged as a powerhouse for CPA (Cost Per Action) affiliates, particularly in the health, wellness, and "nutra" verticals. However, because it is a private network, you don't have a public-facing "marketplace" with a sorted "top offers" list that accurately reflects current Meta ad performance. Many naive affiliates make the mistake of picking an offer based on a high payout figure in the MaxWeb dashboard, only to spend thousands of dollars on ads that fail to convert because the "angle" is burnt out.

The problem is that in CPA marketing, the *offer* is only half the battle; the *angle* (the specific hook used in the ad and bridge page) is what actually drives the ROI. If you approach MaxWeb research by guessing which angles work, you are essentially gambling with your ad spend. A single failed campaign on Meta can cost an operator $500 to $2,000 in "testing" fees before they realize the offer is dead.

By implementing a professional research workflow, you shift from gambling to engineering. Instead of asking "Will this work?", you ask "Who is already making this work, and how can I iterate on their success?" This approach saves hundreds of hours of creative testing and prevents the catastrophic account bans that come from using non-compliant angles.

## Prerequisites and assumptions
Before starting this workflow, you should have an active MaxWeb affiliate account. You cannot run these offers without being approved by the network and having your tracking links generated. You should also have a basic understanding of the Meta Ads Manager and a dedicated "burner" or "agency" account structure to handle the volatility of CPA offers.

Technologically, you will need an ad intelligence tool. While general tools work, this workflow assumes you are looking for high-efficiency data—meaning you want to filter by network, not just by keyword. You should also have a way to host your own landing pages (e.g., ClickFunnels, Elementor, or a custom VPS) because sending traffic directly to a MaxWeb offer link is a fast track to a permanent Meta ban.

## The workflow / framework

### Step 1: Isolate MaxWeb-Specific Traffic
The biggest mistake affiliates make is searching for broad keywords like "Keto" or "Diabetes." This returns millions of results, including organic posts, low-budget amateur ads, and offers from a dozen different networks. To find the real winners, you must filter by the network itself.

You want to use a tool that classifies ads by the affiliate network. By filtering specifically for MaxWeb, you eliminate the noise. You aren't looking for "health ads"; you are looking for "MaxWeb health ads." This allows you to see exactly which offers within the MaxWeb ecosystem are currently receiving the most budget.

When using a classifier, set your filters to "MaxWeb" and "Meta." Look for ads that have been running for at least 10 to 14 days. If an ad has survived the Meta algorithm for two weeks, it is almost certainly generating a positive ROI. If it weren't, the advertiser would have killed it to stop the bleed. This "longevity signal" is the most reliable indicator of a winning offer.

### Step 2: Identify the "Proven Creative" Cluster
Once you have a list of active MaxWeb ads, don't just pick one that looks "cool." Look for clusters. A "Proven Creative" is an image or video that is being used by multiple different affiliates for the same offer. 

If you see five different advertisers all using a similar "weird fruit" image or a specific "doctor's warning" headline for a MaxWeb weight loss offer, you have found a winning angle. This indicates that the creative has been "market-tested" and is consistently converting across different account structures.

Analyze the commonalities. Are they all using a specific color palette? Is the headline formatted as a question or a shocking statement? Is the video a UGC (User Generated Content) style or a high-production animation? Document these patterns. Your goal is not to copy the ad exactly—which can lead to "creative fatigue" and higher CPMs—but to replicate the *psychology* of the winning cluster.

### Step 3: Rip and Analyze the Bridge Page
The ad gets the click, but the bridge page (the landing page between the ad and the offer) gets the conversion. In MaxWeb offers, the bridge page is where the "pre-sell" happens. If you send traffic directly to the VSL (Video Sales Letter), your conversion rate will plummet.

You need to see exactly what the top earners are doing on their bridge pages. This involves "ripping" the landing page—downloading the HTML, CSS, and assets—to examine the flow. You are looking for the "Big Promise," the "Mechanism" (why this product works when others failed), and the "Call to Action."

<ToolLogo slug="adrecon" size="lg" showName />

Using a Landing Page Ripper allows you to take a successful competitor's page and move it into a local environment. Once there, you can analyze the load speed, the mobile responsiveness, and the specific trigger words they use. Note how they transition the user from the "curiosity" of the ad to the "desire" of the offer. If the bridge page uses a quiz format, a fake news article (advertorial), or a personal story, you now know which format is currently converting for that specific MaxWeb offer.

### Step 4: Map the Funnel and Verify Offer Status
Before you spend a single cent on ads, you must verify that the offer is still "live" and accepting traffic. It is common for a spy tool to show an ad that has been running for 30 days, but the offer itself may have been paused by the vendor or capped by MaxWeb.

Click through the entire funnel: Ad $\rightarrow$ Bridge Page $\rightarrow$ Offer Page. Check the offer page for any "Out of Stock" messages or redirects. Ensure the VSL is still playing and the checkout page is functional. 

Additionally, check the MaxWeb dashboard to see if the offer is still available for your account. Some of the highest-converting offers are "private" or require a manual application to the affiliate manager. If you find a winning angle via your research but the offer is locked, this is the perfect time to message your MaxWeb AM. Tell them, "I see [Offer X] is scaling heavily on Meta with [Angle Y], and I have the creative assets ready to go. Can you approve me for this offer?" This shows the AM you are a professional operator, making them much more likely to grant you access.

### Step 5: Iterate and "Angle-Pivot"
Now that you have the winning offer, the winning creative cluster, and the winning bridge page structure, you don't just launch. You iterate. If you launch the exact same assets as the top 5 affiliates, you are competing for the same audience with the same message, which drives up your costs.

Perform an "Angle Pivot." If the winning cluster is using a "Scientific Discovery" angle, try a "Personal Transformation" angle using the same product. Keep the core mechanism of the bridge page (the part that converts) but change the "hook" in the ad.

For example, if the winning ad says "New Tropical Loophole Melts Fat," you might test "Strange Morning Ritual Stops Weight Gain." You are leveraging the proven offer and funnel but creating a unique entry point. This allows you to capture a different segment of the audience and potentially achieve a lower CPA than the original "winner."

## Worked example
Imagine an operator looking for a new health offer on MaxWeb. They start by filtering for "MaxWeb" and "Meta" in their intelligence tool. They notice a surge of ads for a "Blood Sugar Support" supplement. 

The operator sees that 12 different accounts have been running the same "blurred-out image of a common kitchen spice" for 22 days. This is a massive longevity signal. They rip the landing page and discover it's a "Medical News" style advertorial that claims a specific spice is the "missing link" to glucose control.

The operator checks MaxWeb and sees the offer pays $120 per sale. They verify the funnel and find the VSL is still active. Instead of using the "kitchen spice" image, the operator creates a new set of creatives focusing on "hidden sugar traps in breakfast foods," but they keep the "Medical News" bridge page structure because the research proved that format converts. They launch with a $100/day budget, hitting a 2.5x ROAS within the first 48 hours because they started with a proven foundation.

## Pitfalls to avoid

### The "New Ad" Trap
Many beginners see a flashy, high-production ad that looks "modern" and assume it's a winner. They launch it immediately, only to find it's a "test" ad that the original advertiser killed after 48 hours because it failed. Never trust a creative that hasn't been running for at least 10 days. In the world of CPA, "new" usually means "unproven."

### Blind Copy-Pasting
Copying a landing page's HTML is a great way to analyze it, but launching a 1:1 clone is a mistake. Meta's algorithms can detect duplicate landing pages, and if the original advertiser is reporting "clones" to the network, you could get your MaxWeb account banned. Use the ripped page as a blueprint for the *structure* and *copy*, but build your own page with unique images and slightly altered wording.

### Ignoring the "Capping" Issue
Some MaxWeb offers have strict daily caps. If you find a winning offer and scale your spend to $1,000/day, you might find that the vendor only accepts 50 leads per day. Any traffic beyond that is wasted spend. Always ask your affiliate manager about the "daily cap" before scaling a winning campaign.

### Over-Optimizing Too Early
Affiliates often change their ad creative after 24 hours if they don't see a sale. With MaxWeb offers, the "conversion window" can be long. A user might click the ad, read the advertorial, and then buy 6 hours later after watching the VSL. Give your campaigns at least 72 hours of consistent spend before deciding if an angle is a failure.

### Neglecting the "Burn" Rate
Even the best MaxWeb angles eventually "burn." This happens when the target audience has seen the ad too many times, leading to a drop in CTR and a spike in CPA. The sign of a professional operator is knowing when to pivot. When your CPA climbs 30% above your target for three consecutive days, it's time to go back to Step 2 and find a new creative cluster.

## How to know the workflow is working
The primary signal that this workflow is working is a reduction in your "Time to First Sale." Instead of spending a week testing five different offers and ten different angles, you should be able to identify a winning MaxWeb offer and launch a validated angle within 48 hours.

The secondary metric is your "Test-to-Scale Ratio." A naive operator might test 10 offers to find one winner. A professional using this research framework should be able to test 3 offers and find one winner, because the "pre-validation" happened during the research phase. When your CPA is stable and your "days running" on your own ads begins to mirror the longevity of the competitors you researched, you have successfully mastered the MaxWeb research loop.
