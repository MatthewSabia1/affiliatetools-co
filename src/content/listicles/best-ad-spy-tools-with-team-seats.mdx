## Why this ranking exists
In the early days of affiliate marketing, "spying" was a solo sport. You had one account, a few spreadsheets, and you kept your winning creatives a secret. But the game has changed. Modern affiliate operations are now small agencies. You have a media buyer, a creative designer, and perhaps a landing page specialist. Sharing a single login is no longer viable; it leads to session timeouts, security flags, and a total lack of organization.

The problem is that most "team seats" in the SaaS world are just a way for companies to charge you more for the same product. You pay 3x the price for 3 users, but the actual collaboration features—like shared folders, tagging systems, or collaborative briefs—are often non-existent. You're just paying for the privilege of not sharing a password.

Affiliate marketers specifically need a different kind of team support. We don't just need to "save an ad"; we need to categorize ads by affiliate network (ClickBank, MaxWeb, etc.), rip the landing pages for the design team to analyze, and track how long an ad has been running to gauge profitability. A generic "agency plan" that focuses on brand awareness is useless for a CPA operator. This ranking focuses on tools that actually facilitate the affiliate workflow at scale.

## How we ranked
We didn't just look at feature lists. We put these tools through a gauntlet of real-world affiliate scenarios: finding a winning offer on Digistore24, ripping the funnel, and distributing that intelligence across a small team. ### 1. AdRecon — The most cost-effective team solution for Meta-focused affiliate operators
<ToolLogo slug="adrecon" size="lg" showName />

In a typical team workflow, the researcher uses the "Winning Offers Directory" to identify which offers are currently scaling on networks like ClickBank or BuyGoods. Once a winning ad is found, the "Landing Page Ripper" comes into play. Instead of just sending a URL to the designer, the researcher downloads the full HTML/CSS and assets as a ZIP file. This allows the design team to analyze the page structure offline and rebuild a high-converting version without the original page going 404.

Another critical workflow for teams is the "Proven Creatives" grouping. Rather than looking at a single ad, AdRecon groups creatives across different advertisers. This allows a team to see that five different affiliates are all using the same "angle" for a specific nutra offer, signaling a proven market trend rather than a one-off fluke. The "days-running" signal then tells the media buyer exactly how long that ad has been live—if an ad has been running for 30+ days, it's almost certainly profitable.

Compared to Foreplay (our #2 pick), AdRecon is a data-mining tool, not a creative management tool. Foreplay is better for organizing "vibes" and mood boards, but AdRecon is better for finding exactly what is making money on MaxWeb right now and stealing the technical assets of the landing page.

### 2. Foreplay — The gold standard for creative agency collaboration and briefing
<ToolLogo slug="foreplay" size="lg" showName />

Foreplay is less of a "spy tool" in the traditional sense and more of a creative intelligence platform. For agencies with multiple clients and a dedicated creative team, Foreplay's UX is unmatched. Their Agency plan at $199/mo (as of April 2026) is designed specifically to bridge the gap between the media buyer (who finds the ad) and the designer (who makes the new one).

The core workflow in Foreplay centers around "Swipe Files" and "Mood Boards." A media buyer can save a high-performing Meta or TikTok ad into a shared board. From there, they can create a "Creative Brief" directly within the platform. This brief allows the buyer to highlight specific elements of the ad—the hook, the transition, the CTA—and leave notes for the editor. This eliminates the messy process of sending Loom videos and screenshots over Slack.

For teams, Foreplay's ability to save ads permanently is a lifesaver. Most spy tools only show you what is currently active. When an advertiser turns off a winning ad, it usually vanishes from the spy tool. Foreplay saves a permanent copy of the creative, ensuring that your team's research isn't deleted by the advertiser.

However, Foreplay is missing the "affiliate" side of the equation. There is no affiliate-network classification. You can't filter for "all ClickBank offers in the health niche." You have to find the ads manually or through broad keyword searches. Additionally, there is no landing page archiving; you see the ad, but you don't get the full technical breakdown of the funnel.

When compared to AdRecon, Foreplay is significantly more expensive over the long term due to the monthly subscription. But for a high-end agency that bills clients thousands of dollars for creative strategy, the $199/mo (as of April 2026) is a rounding error compared to the time saved in the briefing process.

### 3. BigSpy — The best entry-point for multi-platform teams on a budget
<ToolLogo slug="bigspy" size="lg" showName />

BigSpy is the "Swiss Army Knife" of the group. If your team is just starting out and you aren't sure if your offer will work better on TikTok, Pinterest, or Meta, BigSpy is the most logical choice. Their VIP Enterprise plan at $249/mo (as of April 2026) provides the scale needed for a growing team to monitor multiple platforms simultaneously.

The primary workflow for a BigSpy team is broad-spectrum discovery. A researcher can run a search for "weight loss" and see results across Meta, TikTok, and Google Display. This allows the team to identify which platform is currently "cheaper" or has less competition for a specific niche. Their geographic reach is also superior to most specialists, making it a strong pick for teams running global campaigns.

One of the biggest advantages for teams on a budget is the existence of a real free tier (as of April 2026). This allows a new hire or a junior researcher to get a feel for the tool without the manager having to commit to a high-ticket plan immediately.

The trade-off for this breadth is a lack of depth. BigSpy's data on each platform is "shallower" than that of a specialist. You won't find the same level of granular detail on Meta that you would in AdSpy or AdRecon. Furthermore, like Foreplay, BigSpy lacks affiliate-network classification and landing page archiving. You can see the ad, but you can't easily "rip" the funnel or filter by the specific CPA network.

### 4. PowerAdSpy — Broadest multi-platform coverage, including Native
<ToolLogo slug="poweradspy" size="lg" showName />

PowerAdSpy is the only tool in this list that seriously caters to the Native ad buyer. For teams running Taboola, Outbrain, or RevContent alongside their social campaigns, PowerAdSpy is essential. Their Professional and Premium plans ($149/mo and $249/mo respectively, as of April 2026) are geared toward media buyers who need a panoramic view of the internet.

A typical team workflow here involves "Cross-Channel Validation." A media buyer might find a winning angle on Facebook and then use PowerAdSpy to see if that same angle is working on Native networks. Since Native ads often require a different "editorial" style (more like a news article), being able to spy on Taboola specifically is a huge advantage.

The platform also covers Reddit, Quora, and LinkedIn, which are often ignored by other spy tools. This makes it the best pick for B2B affiliate teams or those running high-ticket offers that require a more professional audience.

The downside is the UX. PowerAdSpy feels dated. It doesn't have the slickness of Foreplay or the streamlined focus of AdRecon. It's a database first and a user experience second. There is also no affiliate-network classification, meaning you'll spend a lot of time manually vetting whether an ad is for a CPA offer or a direct-to-consumer brand.

### 5. AdSpy — The 10-year Meta ad archive with Boolean search
<ToolLogo slug="adspy" size="lg" showName />

AdSpy is the "old guard" of the industry. While it lacks the modern team collaboration features of Foreplay or the lifetime pricing of AdRecon, it possesses something no one else has: a massive, 10-year historical database of Meta ads. At $149/mo (as of April 2026), it's a steep recurring cost, but for data scientists in the affiliate space, it's the gold standard.

The power-user workflow in AdSpy revolves around Boolean search. A senior researcher can use operators like AND, OR, and NOT to carve out incredibly specific segments of the market. For example, they can search for ads that contain "Free Shipping" AND "Weight Loss" but NOT "Keto." This level of precision allows a team to find "hidden" winners that simpler filters would miss.

For a team, AdSpy is best used as a research library. You don't go there to manage your creative workflow; you go there to find the "ancestor" of a current trend. By looking at what worked three years ago, a team can often predict what will work again in the next cycle.

The weaknesses are glaring in 2026. There is no landing page archiving, no affiliate-network classification, and the UX is clunky. It's a tool for people who love data and hate "fluff." If your team needs a modern workspace, AdSpy will feel like using a library card catalog in the age of Google.

### 6. PiPiADS — The TikTok ad specialist
<ToolLogo slug="pipiads" size="lg" showName />

If your team's primary acquisition channel is TikTok, PiPiADS is non-negotiable. While other tools "cover" TikTok, PiPiADS lives there. Their VIP plan at $155/mo (as of April 2026) provides the deepest possible insight into the TikTok Creative Center and beyond.

The team workflow here is all about "Trend Riding." TikTok moves faster than any other platform. A winning creative can go viral and die within two weeks. PiPiADS allows a team to spot these spikes in real-time. The integration with TikTok's own data allows researchers to see exactly which hashtags and sounds are driving conversions.

However, PiPiADS is very weak on Meta. If you're trying to run a multi-channel campaign, you'll find the Meta data disappointing. Like most of the multi-platform tools, it lacks affiliate-network classification and landing page ripping. It's a specialized tool for a specialized platform.

### 7. Minea — Multi-platform ad spy tuned for dropshipping
<ToolLogo slug="minea" size="lg" showName />

Minea is an incredible tool, but it's built for a different animal: the dropshipper. Their Business plan at €399/mo (as of April 2026) is designed for teams that are sourcing physical products from China and selling them via Shopify.

The standout feature for Minea teams is the supplier research integration. Once a team finds a winning product ad on TikTok or Pinterest, Minea helps them find the supplier for that product. This creates a seamless workflow from "Spy" to "Source" to "Sale."

For a CPA affiliate, however, Minea is less useful. We don't care about suppliers; we care about offer owners and affiliate networks. Minea doesn't provide affiliate-network classification, and its focus on DTC (Direct-to-Consumer) e-commerce means it misses a lot of the "weird" CPA offers that make the real money in the affiliate world.

## What we left off — and why
You'll notice we didn't include a lot of the "AI Ad Generators" that have popped up recently. We consciously left them off because they aren't spy tools; they are creative tools. Finding a winning ad is a research task; generating a new one is a creative task. We believe in keeping those two functions separate.

We also excluded several "free" Chrome extensions. While they are great for a quick peek at a competitor's ad library, they don't offer team seats, shared databases, or historical archiving. They are toys, not tools. For a professional team, a browser extension is a supplement, not a strategy.
